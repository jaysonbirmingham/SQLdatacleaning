SQL Data Cleaning Project¶

In This project, I use SQL to clean a salary dataset obtained from:¶

https://www.askamanager.org/2021/04/how-much-money-do-you-make-4.html.

Users enter professional information into a questionnaire. Some answers require users to check one answer out of several factor levels, while other answers allow users to input their own text. This creates some very messy data.¶

Industry¶

The next column, Industry, has almost 500 distinct values. The questionnaire offered significantly less options than that, but there was an option at the bottom for user-input text. People went crazy with this one.¶

I want this data to be usable for factor analysis, especially within this category. My goal here is to aggregate the distinct values of industry together as best I can.¶

In [49]:
%%sql
SELECT COUNT(DISTINCT Industry) AS 'Number of Unique Industry Entries'
FROM survey;

A quick inspection of the counts of each industry value reveal that many of the distinct industry values are simply misspellings and misrenderings of similar industries. I can aggregate these together by using the LIKE function in SQL and labeling them with a corrected format.¶

While this method is easier than changing each industry by hand, it is by no means the most efficient way.¶

A quicker method would have been to create a new column with the LAG of the industry column, and then run a DIFFERENCE function between the two. If the difference value equals 4 (meaning they are phonetically identical), then I could aggregate them under the same Industry label. Since SQLite does not offer these functions, this is something I will do in a future project.¶

After having spent a considerable amount of time using those CASE statments, I wanted to lump all of the stragglers into the category of 'OTHER'. If I wanted to spend another few hours classifying each row individually, I could have. However, for the sake of time, they are getting clumped together.¶

Here's a double-nested subquery to update those values.¶

In [58]:
%%sql

UPDATE survey
SET Industry = 'Other'
WHERE Industry NOT IN
    (SELECT CASE
    WHEN Count >= 5 THEN Industry
    WHEN Count < 5 THEN 'Other'
    END AS Industry
FROM (SELECT Industry, COUNT(Industry) AS Count
        FROM survey
        GROUP BY Industry))

I did notice that many jobs had Roman numerals behind them, which I trimmed off. This would aggregate a few of the titles.¶

Other inconsistencies came from word abbreviations, such as shortening Director to Dir, or Administrator to Admin. I used the REPLACE function to format some of these. Like I said, SQLite is limited in text formatting. This column will be saved for another project when I'll use PostgreSQL.¶

After reviewing the high outliers, some things became apparent. For one, many of the high numbers are from currencies other than the US dollar. Due to exchange rates, this is not surprising.
